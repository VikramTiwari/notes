# 🏎️ Hyper-Growth Cash Burn

Throwing large amounts of venture capital at a business model with unproven unit economics and low margins is a recipe for a fast collapse.

---

## Fast (YC S20)
* **What they built**: A 1-click checkout button for independent e-commerce sites. The software stored user payment details so you could buy items across different websites without creating new accounts or typing in credit card numbers.
* **The Failure**: Fast suffered from the ultimate two-sided network problem. To get shoppers to use it, they needed merchants to install the button. To get merchants to install it, they needed shoppers to already be using it. To force this network into existence, Fast raised over &#36;120M and engaged in a legendary cash-burning spree—hiring aggressively, sponsoring NASCAR drivers, and throwing massive parties. Meanwhile, their actual software was highly commoditized, and competitors like Shop Pay (Shopify) and Apple Pay already owned the distribution.
* **The Outcome**: In 2022, news leaked that despite spending tens of millions of dollars, their annual revenue was reportedly around &#36;600,000. They ran out of money and abruptly shut down, serving as a cautionary tale that massive marketing budgets cannot fix fundamentally flawed unit economics in software.
---

## Atrium (YC W18)
* **What they built**: Atrium started as a tech-enabled law firm built by Justin Kan (who previously sold YC-backed Twitch). When they realized that operating a service business with actual lawyers had terrible margins, they laid off their legal staff and pivoted to become a pure B2B legal software (SaaS) platform for startups.
* **The Failure**: They had raised over &#36;75M from top-tier investors based on the narrative that they were going to "disrupt the legal industry." But when they pivoted to pure software, they were suddenly just another legal CRM competing in a saturated, slow-moving market. The math simply broke down: they had the massive cash burn and growth expectations of a hyper-growth unicorn, but the actual revenue trajectory of a standard B2B SaaS tool. Medium
* **The Outcome**: Realizing that the pure software revenue could never grow fast enough to justify the venture capital they had raised, they abruptly shut down in 2020 and returned the remaining money to investors. Medium
---

### 💡 Key Takeaway
For startups in this category, the core challenge is not the code but the surrounding market dynamics. Ensure you validate this bottleneck before scaling.
