# ⏳ Too Early / Pre-Infrastructure

Sometimes the product vision is correct, but the surrounding hardware or internet infrastructure is not mature or cheap enough to support it.

---

## NewsTilt (YC W10)
* **What they built**: A publishing platform and CMS designed to help traditional print journalists transition to the web, build independent brands, and monetize their own audiences. It was essentially the exact premise of Substack, but built in 2010.
* **The Failure**: They built the software without truly understanding the user. In a famously candid post-mortem, the founders admitted that they weren't journalists and didn't really enjoy talking to journalists. They assumed that if they built a beautiful writing tool with built-in SEO and ad networks, the journalists would succeed. But they completely missed the actual problem: journalists didn't need a better text editor; they needed an audience. NewsTilt couldn't drive traffic to the writers, and the writers couldn't drive enough traffic on their own to survive.
* **The Outcome**: The founders lost passion for the problem, ran out of momentum, and shut the software down just a few months after their YC batch.
---

## Nitrous.io (YC S12)
* **What they built**: A pure software cloud-based Integrated Development Environment (IDE). The thesis was that developers shouldn't have to waste hours setting up local environments on their MacBooks. Nitrous provided a full development environment in the browser—you just logged in and started coding on their servers instantly.
* **The Failure**: They accurately predicted the future (today, tools like GitHub Codespaces and Cursor are massive), but they were a decade too early. In 2012–2016, cloud infrastructure was still relatively expensive, and browser technologies weren't quite fast enough to make typing feel as instantaneous as a local text editor. Furthermore, developers are notoriously cheap; convincing them to pay a monthly subscription for a cloud environment when their local laptop could do it for free was an impossible sales hurdle.
* **The Outcome**: After raising nearly &#36;7 million, the server costs outpaced their revenue from paying developers. They ran out of cash and abruptly shut down all operations in 2016, permanently deleting all user data hosted on their servers.
---

## Easel (YC W12)
* **What they built**: A pure software, in-browser web design tool. Long before Figma dominated the market, Easel attempted to bring the entire web design process out of heavy, offline desktop apps (like Adobe Photoshop) and into a collaborative, cloud-native browser environment.
* **The Failure**: They accurately predicted the future of design, but the underlying browser technology in 2012 was incredibly difficult to optimize for heavy, pixel-perfect graphic rendering. Convincing designers to abandon their deeply entrenched offline workflows for a web app that occasionally lagged proved to be an uphill battle. The software couldn't quite achieve the instantaneous, native feel that Figma eventually cracked years later by utilizing newer browser technologies like WebGL.
* **The Outcome**: Recognizing they were struggling to hit venture-scale growth as a standalone product, they were acquired by GitHub in 2014. GitHub primarily wanted the engineering team to overhaul their own internal UI, and the standalone Easel software was subsequently shut down.
---

### 💡 Key Takeaway
For startups in this category, the core challenge is not the code but the surrounding market dynamics. Ensure you validate this bottleneck before scaling.
